    Two Voyagers, Two Witnesses: Poets Theo Dorgan and Dennis O’Driscoll

    Joe Heininger, Professor, English

    The contemporary Irish poet Dennis O’Driscoll’s journeys unfold in physical space and ordinary time and in an eschatological or crisis time where we live without “our one true God” who is reckoned as “dead,” as in his poem, “Missing God.” O’Driscoll vividly portrays emotional cliffs and unfamiliar roads together with stifled emotions and the fear of redundancy that can be so prevalent in work environments that it affects private and home life. Another contemporary Irish poet, Theo Dorgan, ranges over the seas with poems about voyagers and their personal discoveries. He also meditates on love and death, especially in a powerful lyric poem written in response to Cesare Pavese (“Death will come and have your eyes”). My talk will look at examples of their most engaging and moving artistic witness

    M&Ms & M—Way of Being, i.e. (Mazzuchelli & Mission & the Mound – Way of Being)

    Peter Alonzi, Professor, Brennan School of Business

    The Mound is special, sacred space. Father Samuel Mazzuchelli, O.P. chose the Mound as the place for founding his American Dominican Province and as the birthplace of the Sinsinawa Dominican Congregation of the Most Holy Rosary of the Order of Preachers. For us too it is special, sacred. It is the source and north star of Dominican University. For nearly a year the gigantic pandemic wave has robbed us of the opportunity to BE at the Mound in person, to experience the Way of Being lived by the Sisters at the Mound today, to tour the “Telling Our Story” exhibit of the first Sisters, to experience the exhibit relating the life of Father Samuel Mazzuchelli, O.P. from his birth in 1806 in Milan to his death in Benton Wisconsin in 1864. To offset, in part, this stolen opportunity we can use our imaginations. To overcome this pandemic wave, or at least to surf it, we can use our Zoom surf boards to take a virtual tour of the Mound’s Father Samuel exhibit “Tracing the Journey”. The tour brings Father Samuel to life by illustrating the telling of his life’s journey with many beautiful photographs (professional grade) of the exhibit shared by the Sinsinawan Sisters. My narration draws upon essential sources: many conversations over two decades with more than three score of Sisters, the biography of Father Samuel American Dominican written by Sr. Mary Nona McGreal, O,P, Father Samuel’s own Memoirs, and numerous visits to the exhibit beginning with Sr. Priscilla Wood’s telling of the Fr. Samuel’s story and then many later visits alone, dwelling in Fr. Samuel’s exhibit and pondering. The tour provides many Facts of Fr. Samuel’s life so that we come to know but even more that we come to understand Fr. Samuel’s Mission, and yet still even more that we appreciate his Way of Being. See you on the tour. Surf’s up..

  19. Tuesday, February 2

    A Holistic Approach to Racial Healing with TRHT & ASSYS: Truth, Racial Healing & Transformation

    Joyce Mojica, Counselor and Ami Omi, University Minister

    (TRHT) is a comprehensive, national and community-based process to bring about transformational and sustainable change, and to address the historic and contemporary effects of racism. Acute Stress Syndrome Stabilization (ASSYST©) is a procedure that can be used to address the effects of racial trauma through AIP-informed psychophysiological algorithmic approaches specially designed for persons experiencing distressing memories and symptoms related to trauma. Amy Omi, University Minister and Joyce Mojica, Staff Therapist in Wellness will explain the TRHT framework as a foundational approach to Racial Healing and Relationship building and will share how the (ASSYST©) is an interactive group experience that allows people to process stress nonverbally with others who are also experiencing similar stresses. This collaboration will highlight ways for all of us to heal from the wounds of the past, to build mutually respectful relationships across racial and ethnic lines and heal from the effects of racial trauma.

    Resources for Teaching About the Pandemic From the Perspective of Biology

    Scott Kreher, Chair, Biology Department

    The COVID-19 pandemic has affected all aspects of our lives and there are multiple lenses to view causes and effects. We will discuss the pandemic from the perspective of biology, and provide resources grounded in science for teaching about this coronavirus, SARS-CoV-2, and vaccines. We will also present useful examples for comparison of other coronavirus-caused diseases in other animals, including cats and dogs. Finally, we will present better frameworks for understanding and preventing zoonotic diseases (diseases which originate in other animals) and place the pandemic in the context of better conservation policies.

    The McGreal Center for Dominican Historical Studies

    Chris Allison, Director of the McGreal Center of Dominican Historical Studies

    The McGreal Center for Dominican Historical Studies is a research center and archive on campus devoted to the study of the Dominican Order’s past in the United States. While many of the faculty and staff will be familiar with the center, this conversation will introduce you to the mission of the center and our research initiatives. It will also focus on ways that a historical collection can enhance your pedagogical goals by getting students directly involved in historical research. Working with collections is a special opportunity to help students develop close reading skills, intellectual focus, information literacy, and conduct vocational discernment by exposing them to career opportunities in collections, libraries, archives, museums, or universities. In a time when history is fresh on the minds of many at Dominican University, the McGreal center is happy to be a partner in facilitating discussions around our past, if in your courses or beyond.

  10. Thursday, November 5

    Anti-Racism Research

    Sheila Radford-Hill, Lisa Petrov, Aliza Steurer

    In light of the recent racial protests and violence in Chicago neighborhoods, it is time to have some honest conversations about how we can support our colleagues of color and our students in our classes and beyond. We want this particular conversation to increase our understanding and our commitment to pursuing anti-racist policies and practices on our campus and, to the extent possible, in the communities where our students live and work. Throughout Dominican's history, the Sinsinawa Dominican sisters and many of us have participated in making our campus more just for all students, but we know there is more work to do. We want to discuss what to do, when, and how we are going to become a more equitable campus. Ground rules for the conversation will be provided, along with discussion prompts and pre-reads, so that participants feel safe expressing their thoughts and we can all learn from the conversation.

    Article “Dear White People…”: This article provides background information for an activity that participants will engage in during the conversation. Additionally, the article has a strong and jarring message, provides concrete steps that faculty can take to support their colleagues of color, and acknowledges that people will make mistakes when doing antiracist work (which can be reassuring).

    Blog post on Black Lives Matter from the Mathematical Association of America: This source is an example of how a discipline-specific professional organization is addressing equity, so that faculty in different disciplines can look to their professional organizations for guidance. The blog post also provides concrete steps for how a department can take to be antiracist.

    Dear White People: Reimagining Whiteness In the Struggle for Racial Equity
